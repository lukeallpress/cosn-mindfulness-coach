What it would take to build this well

The deeper detail behind the prototype — how the assistant would work, what makes its feedback trustworthy, and the infrastructure, cost, and governance involved. Written for a tech-savvy, non-developer audience.

In one line

A guided assistant grounded in CoSN's own guidance

A leader or educator describes a lesson, policy question, or screen-time concern and chooses a mode (e.g., Leader Policy Support). The assistant returns tailored, framework-based feedback and next steps — anchored to CoSN's research and positions, not the open internet. The interface is already live; the AI layer is intentionally stubbed in the prototype. The points below describe what a real build requires.

How it works

"Retrieve, then reason"

  1. Retrieve. The tool pulls the most relevant passages from a curated library of CoSN reports, toolkits, and policy guidance — so answers are rooted in approved material rather than a model's general training.
  2. Reason against the framework. It applies CoSN's mindfulness framework (Purpose · Learning Value · Safety · Reflection) as an explicit rubric.
  3. Return a structured plan. It produces a snapshot and concrete next steps for the specific situation — supporting local judgment, not replacing it.

This "retrieve-then-reason" pattern (often called RAG — retrieval-augmented generation) is the standard way to keep an AI assistant on-message and citable.

The real work

Making the feedback trustworthy

This is content and editorial work as much as engineering — and it's where CoSN's expertise is essential:

  • Curate the source material. Decide exactly which CoSN reports, toolkits, and guidance the assistant may draw from.
  • Define the rubric. Turn the framework into explicit criteria the assistant scores against, so its reasoning is consistent and explainable.
  • Calibrate against experts. Run real scenarios, compare the assistant's feedback to what a CoSN expert would say, and tune until they reliably agree.
  • Set guardrails. Instruct it to defer to local context, flag "verify locally," cite its sources, and stay within scope.
  • Keep it current. As CoSN's guidance evolves, the library and rubric are updated. This is an ongoing product, not a one-time document.
Why this matters most: the output will carry CoSN's name. Calibration is what separates a credible CoSN resource from a generic chatbot.

Infrastructure

Hosting and "powering" it

The interface A static web page (what you're viewing). Essentially free to host and already live on GitHub Pages.
The "brain" A commercial AI model (e.g., Anthropic's Claude or OpenAI) accessed via an API. CoSN holds the account and pays per use.
The knowledge layer A searchable store of the curated CoSN materials the model retrieves from — a low-cost managed service.
Governance plumbing Access control, a clear data-handling policy (especially if districts upload local context), and a privacy/vendor review.

Budget shape

Costs are modest, but recurring

  • Hosting: negligible for the interface.
  • Per-use AI: typically a fraction of a cent to a few cents per question, so cost scales gently with adoption.
  • Knowledge store: low monthly cost.
  • The ongoing investment is people: periodic content curation and re-calibration as guidance changes.

Rough figures for planning only — a pilot would produce real numbers.

Governance & privacy

Decisions to make up front

  • Scope: what the assistant is allowed to say, and where it must defer.
  • Data handling: whether district-provided context is stored, how it's protected, and what the AI vendor may retain (most offer no-training / no-retention options).
  • Sign-off: who reviews and approves the curated library and the assistant's behavior.

None of this is exotic — but it should be decided deliberately, not bolted on later.

Where things stand

Prototype status

Not yet   The connected, calibrated AI layer: curated CoSN library, expert-tuned rubric, guardrails, and governance.

Recommended next step

A small, funded pilot

Connect the existing logic to a curated CoSN knowledge base, test it against several real scenarios with expert review, and bring the results back to the task force. That produces real evidence on quality, effort, and cost before any larger commitment.
